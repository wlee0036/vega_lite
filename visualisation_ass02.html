Gender Differences in Suicide

Women are more likely to be diagnosed with suicidal thoughts,
but men died from suicide at a higher rate

In all countries, the rate of men suicides are greater than women suicides. Although women are well-known for higher rates of depression diagnoses as well as suicidal thoughts, men happen to die more from suicides.


Several factors contribute to this phenomena. One key element is the lack of communication. Society has, for many generations, encourage men to stay strong and not to cast out their emotions as it is believed as a sign of weakness. As a result, men are less likely to seek help when facing emotional problems. Most of them self-treat their depression with alcohol, drugs and other substances. Looks like the 'boys don't cry' pressure is causing damaging mental health consequences.


Moreover, in a lot of culture, men are the head of the household. They worry more about finances or trying to find a job. When there are economic factors they can't control, it becomes very difficult. In fact, a study in 2015 found that for every 1% increase in unemployment there is a 0.79% increase in suicide rate.


Men suicides are highest in European countries. Gender disparity in suicide rate are also the biggest in this region. Meanwhile women suicides are highest in Asian countries. As can be seen, countries like Sri Lanka, Japan, Republic of Korea lead the rate of women suicide globally.

Since 1985 to 2016, there has been slightly decreasing trend in women suicide rate. On the other hand, men experienced a major spike during early 90s, reaching the peak at 1995 with approximately 1 out of every 4000 population committing suicide annually. The trend has then been decreasing steadily with better awareness and suicide prevention strategy by the government. Data for the unprecedented year 2020 would be interesting to look at to see if the trend continues given the tough time.

Suicide Rate by Age

Suicide rate increases with age

Generally, the likelihood of suicide increases with a person's age. This might looks surprising given the fact that suicide is one of the leading causes of death among the young people.


So, why do suicide rate are highest among the 75+ years age group? Well, this is actually because people aged 70 years above died from numerous reasons: cardiovascular disease, cancer, stroke, diabetes, Alzheimer's disease, and respiratory diseases. The number of suicide is high, but still generally surpassed by other causes.


The pattern shows that since the year 1995, suicide rate for everyone aged 15 and above has been constantly decreasing. A significant drop in suicide rate happened in the age group 75+ years. Since 1995, this rate has fall by more than 30%. Suicide rate in the 5-14 category remains roughly stable and small with under 1 suicide per 100,000 population per year.


Globally, in every continent, the trend of suicide rate increasing with age is true. However, this is not the case in Africa and Oceania. In Africa, the age group 35-54 years has the highest suicide rate. Meanwhile, in Oceania the age group 15-24 years has the highest suicide rate. This might be due to underrepresentation of countries in both continents, only data of 3 countries were available for Africa and only Australia and New Zealand represents Oceania.

How Have Suicide Rates Changed?

The 45 degree grey line represents parity. Countries which lie along
this line had the same suicide rate in 2015 as they did in 1995.
Countries which lie above this line had higher rates in 1995, but have improved recently.

Country with the highest increase

Republic of Korea




According to WHO, Korea is currently in the top 10 countries for
suicide rate, leading the OECD countries. Around 36 people per day
commited suicide. In Korea, people receive high social pressure ever
since they are a kid; academic/school pressures as well as the strict
parenting culture. The entry requirements to top-tier universities and
corporations are rigid. Society has also put beauty standards, judging
people's worth by how they look.

Country with the greatest decline

Estonia




Estonia is a country situated in Northern Europe, surrounded by Russia
to the east, and by Latvia to the south. Estonia is a developed country,
ranking quite high in the Human Development Index. The country used
to have a very high suicide rate, one of the highest in Europe mainly
due to high alcohol consumption and Lutheran ethics. Despite its rate
still being considerably high, Estonia has improved the most globally.

Can Money Buy Happiness?

Is there any link between how rich a country is
and how high the suicide rate?

There is no clear relationship between
GDP per capita and suicide rate

The visualization on the right tells us that there appears to be
no statistically significant relationship between GDP per capita and
suicide rate. The relationship might be a little bit prominent
in specific continent like the Europe, but again this pattern is
not always seen as year varies.


The notion that a country's suicide rate decreases as it gets wealthier
is not true. In fact, we can see that suicide rate is a problem faced
in all countries globally, regardless of whether they are rich or not.


For example, Australia, a country which has been growing rapidly
in terms of GDP per capita for the last 20 years. In 2015,
Australia's GDP per capita is approximately 60,656 almost
triple times than its GDP per capita in 1995. Despite that
being said, its suicide rate has been slightly higher.


Although money is believed by many as a factor that drives people
to take their own lives, there are a lot of other socioeconomic
factors to take into consideration to solve this complex puzzle.
